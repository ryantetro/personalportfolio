02 — Philosophy
Design Principles

Marc writes his own design rules. These are the principles extracted from his newsletter, products, and public posts.

Rule 01
Conversion over creativity
Every design decision must funnel users toward one action. No decorative choices for their own sake. A beautiful page that doesn't convert is a failure.
Rule 02
3 colors max (like beers)
"Colors are like beers. Things get messy after 3 or 4." Marc's own rule — use a base, a primary CTA color, and muted text. That's it.
Rule 03
One CTA. Always.
Landing pages that convert have 1 main call-to-action. Secondary links exist but should never compete visually with the primary CTA button.
Rule 04
Dark-first aesthetic
Almost all Marc's products use a dark theme — deep navy or near-black backgrounds. Orange as a primary accent for energy and urgency. This became his signature look.
Rule 05
Ship-able > Perfect
Design is a tool to get profitable fast. Good enough to convert + shipped beats perfect but delayed. Marc avoids complex design patterns that slow him down.
Rule 06
Consistent across products
Each product has the same footer cross-linking all other products. Same font stack. Same color logic. Users who buy one product recognize the family instantly.
03 — Colors
The Color System

Marc's formula: a deep dark base + one punchy primary color + muted text hierarchy. No gradients on text. No pure black.

Marc uses DaisyUI (Tailwind plugin) for all ShipFast projects. The theme maps to this system. He avoids pure #000000 — always uses soft dark grays or midnight blue.

04 — Typography
Type System

Marc uses DaisyUI defaults plus Google Fonts. Headlines are bold, punchy, and short. Body text is readable and restrained.

Display / Headlines
Sora
Heavy weight (700/800), tight letter-spacing (-0.02 to -0.04em). Used for H1, H2, pricing numbers, product names.
Body / UI Text
DM Sans
Clean, geometric sans. Used for paragraphs, buttons, nav. 400 weight for paragraphs, 600–700 for buttons.
Monospace / Labels / Code
DM Mono
Used for section labels, price tags, badges, code blocks. Often in uppercase with letter-spacing.

FAQ accordion after pricing

Problem → Solution → Features → Proof
The page narrative follows: Hero (promise) → Problem visualization (pain) → Solution overview (relief) → Feature details (proof it works) → Social proof (others succeeded) → Pricing (act now) → FAQ (remove objections). This sequence appears in every flagship site.

3-step problem visualization
Multiple sites (CodeFast, HabitsGarden, HeroFit, Insighto, PoopUp) use a 3-step emoji/icon flow showing the problem: Set goal → Try your best → Life doesn't change. Connected by arrows. "80% fail" statistic appears in 4 sites. Creates shared emotional hook.
Pattern 09
One accent color per product
Every product has exactly ONE accent color. Never two. The accent is used for: CTA buttons, progress bar, badges, section labels, hover states. The dark background + single accent creates instant brand identity. 14 distinct accent colors across 30+ products.
Pattern 10
NProgress loading bar
Every Next.js site uses NProgress — a thin colored bar at the very top of the viewport that animates during page transitions. Always colored in the product's accent color. A small detail that makes the site feel like a real app, not a static page.
Pattern 11
Tool-first pages for free products
Free tools (LogoFast, BooksCalculator, Mood2Movie, DecisionGame) skip the marketing hero entirely — the tool loads immediately. No persuasion needed. These tools then cross-sell paid products via embedded cards. The tool IS the marketing.
Pattern 12
DaisyUI + Tailwind everywhere
Nearly every site uses DaisyUI's component classes (btn, card, badge, tooltip, accordion) on top of Tailwind utilities. The "dark" theme is default. Some sites override with custom DaisyUI themes. This gives every site the same baseline quality without custom CSS.
Why these 30+ sites don't look "vibe-coded" — the specific decisions
What Marc does (copy these)
  • Hero ALWAYS has 5 layers: badge → H1 → subtitle → CTA → avatar proof. Never just H1 + button.
  • ONE accent color per product — used for CTA, progress bar, badges, labels. Nothing else is colored.
  • Testimonials always include what the person built ("built X.com") — credibility, not just praise.
  • Featured pricing tier has visual differentiation: colored border, "POPULAR" badge, subtle bg gradient.
  • Social proof numbers are oddly specific (8,186 not 8,000+) — feels like live data.
  • Feature sections use visual grouping (tabs, grids, timelines) — never a bullet list wall.
  • Section padding is generous: py-20 to py-24 minimum (80-96px). Sections breathe.
  • Footer cross-links every product — turns visitors into ecosystem nodes.
  • CTA button text = action verb + product name + arrow: "Get ShipFast →"
  • Problem section before features: show pain first, then relief.
  • NProgress loading bar in accent color makes page transitions feel app-like.
  • "Made with ☕ and 🥐 by Marc" — consistent personal brand in every footer.
What vibe-coded sites get wrong (avoid these)
  • Hero with just H1 + button — no social proof layer, no badge, no subtitle.
  • Multiple competing accent colors on one page — kills visual hierarchy.
  • Testimonials with just "I loved it!" — no name, no context, no outcome.
  • All pricing tiers look identical — no visual distinction for the recommended tier.
  • Social numbers rounded ("8k+ users") — feels like marketing fluff.
  • Features listed as bullet points under one H2 — no cards, no icons, no structure.
  • Sections crammed together with py-4 or py-8 — feels like a Google Doc.
  • Footer with just "© 2025" — missed opportunity for discovery and cross-sell.
  • CTA says "Buy" or "Sign up" — generic, no product name, no arrow.
  • Features listed immediately after hero — no emotional hook or problem statement.
  • No loading state or transition animation — feels like a static HTML file.
  • No founder section — faceless product feels like a template demo.

The conversion formula: Marc's pages never sell in the features section — they sell in the pricing section. Everything before pricing (problem → features → proof → founder) is building desire and trust. Everything after pricing (FAQ) is removing objections. CTAs exist only at the top (hero) for impulse buyers and at pricing for considered buyers. The absence of CTAs in content sections is what makes the page feel like education, not a sales pitch.
